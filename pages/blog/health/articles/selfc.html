What is Self-Confidence?

Date: November 1, 2023 | Author: Julia Mbamarah

When you walk into an exam hall confident, you're going to ace the test, you have self-confidence. A person is confident when they believe they can do a task successfully by using their judgment, knowledge, and past experiences.

Self-confidence is an attitude about your skills and abilities. It implies that you accept and trust yourself, and that you have a sense of control over your life. You are aware of your own talents and weaknesses and have a good self-image. You have the ability to set realistic objectives and goals, speak assertively, and take criticism.

Low self-confidence, on the other hand, may cause you to question yourself, be weak or subservient, or have difficulties trusting others. You may feel inferior, neglected, or easily offended. Feeling confidence in oneself could depend on the scenario. For instance, you can feel very confident in some areas, such as academics, but lack confidence in others, like relationships.

Self-confidence is rarely related to actual abilities and is mostly based on your perceptions. Perceptions are the way you think about yourself and these thoughts can be flawed. Growing up in an unsupportive and critical atmosphere, being away from friends or family for the first time, evaluating oneself too harshly, or being terrified of failure can all lead to low self-confidence. People who lack self-confidence frequently make mistakes in their reasoning.

Where does self-confidence come from?

Two types of confidence exist:

  1. Intrinsic Confidence
  2. Extrinsic Confidence

1. Intrinsic Confidence

Intrinsic confidence is equivalent to self-assurance. It means that your confidence comes from within and that you are not reliant on others to give it to you. Confidence originates from inside and is fueled by your accomplishments and pleasure. Contrary to common assumption, self-assurance is not necessarily innate. According to one poll, just one in ten respondents believe their self-confidence is inherent. Others believe that their self-assurance is derived from other sources. This demonstrates how difficult it is to create genuine inherent confidence. Few people have the mental fortitude to do so, and those who do must train for years. When a person can embrace their accomplishments and look up to themselves as someone who should be admired, they are on the road to developing intrinsic self-confidence.

2. Extrinsic Confidence

Extrinsic confidence is when you rely on others to give you confidence. People around you make you think that you are deserving of what you have accomplished and motivate you to do even more. Extrinsic self-esteem is self-esteem that comes from somewhere other than oneself. In other words, it is the kind of self-esteem that comes from outside sources such as other people, praises, accomplishments, or acclaim. When you need short-term motivation, external confidence comes in handy. You can always rely on people to encourage you for a limited time. It is considerably easier and requires less effort to create inner confidence. While there is nothing wrong with relying on others, extrinsic confidence is not always sufficient. If people don't believe in us, no matter how much we want to, we begin to doubt ourselves. It is never good to rely solely on people for anything, let alone your source of happiness. It will always be difficult to locate people on whom you can rely for consistent inspiration.
