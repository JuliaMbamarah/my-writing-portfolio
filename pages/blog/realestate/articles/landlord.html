Solutions to Common Landlord-Tenant Problems

You, the landlord, are aware of the many challenges that lie ahead. Tenant management may be challenging, especially when dealing with problematic renters. Whether you're an experienced landlord or not, you can easily learn how to better handle the most common complaints from tenants. Here are some solutions to common landlord-tenant problems.

Late or Non-Payment of Rent

Late payment or no payment at all is one of the biggest problems that arise between landlords and tenants. Most landlords say that renters who pay their rent late or who don't pay at all are the most frustrating tenants to deal with. Tenants who don't pay their rent on time or at all may have valid excuses. There might have been difficulties in paying the rent, disagreements with the landlord, or simple forgetfulness. However, like with so many other issues, effective communication is necessary for both parties to find a solution. To prevent this kind of problem, always verify that the renter has been made aware of the total amount owed and the date by which it is due. If your rent is consistently late, you should have a conversation with your tenant about the issue. You can even try to be of help by providing a payment plan that works within their budget.

Issues With Maintenance

Make sure the lease explicitly states the responsibilities of each party. On frequent occasions, landlords often pay for necessary repairs. If a tenant approaches you about a maintenance issue, for which you are responsible, you should respond quickly. Assure them that their feedback has been heard and that they will be updated as the situation evolves.

Appliance Failures

If regular appliance maintenance and repairs are part of your contract's terms, you must uphold your end of the agreement. All of the property's appliances will need to be maintained by you. If any of the appliances, whether or not they were included with the property, break during the lease term, the renter is responsible for the expense of repair or replacement. However, this does not mean that broken appliances should be disregarded and tenants permitted in. Look around the inside and outside of the house for any evidence of damage. It is essential to fix any broken appliances before a new renter moves in.

Breaching Agreement

The lease agreement contains the rules that the tenant must follow. Restrictions on things like noise, dogs, and curfews all fall under this category. Regrettably, some tenants disagree that the rules are necessary but most tenants are generally kind people who never cause any trouble. However, even the most careful tenants sometimes forget something or damage something. Tenants who break the rules should be given a written warning specifying when the issue must be resolved. Any penalty for failing to comply such as eviction should be made crystal clear.

Property Damage

The most upsetting issues that might arise between landlords and tenants are related to vandalism. Unfortunately, some tenants may intentionally cause damage to your property in the days leading up to their departure. Even worse, they may pretend nothing has happened and attribute damage to the condition they met it in. If you want to prevent this problem, you need to start taking action right away. Do a walkthrough inspection of the property before renting it out. Collect a signed photo release from the tenant verifying the rental's condition. After the renter has moved out, you should re-inspect the property and take new photos as evidence of its condition. Time and date stamps should be put on all images taken during the move-in and move-out inspections.

Overdue Utility Bills

Tenant and landlord disagreements over utility costs are not uncommon. In your leasing agreement, you need to specify who is responsible for what expenses. Utilities may be in the landlord's name, but even if the lease holds the tenant liable for payment, the landlord cannot enforce payment.

Why Tenant Screening is Important

Tenant-landlord conflicts are a common cause of stress during a tenancy. The good news is that most of these problems may be avoided with thorough tenant screening. Problematic tenants are individuals who are late with their rent payments, cause numerous maintenance issues, repeatedly break the conditions of their lease, and leave without paying their last energy bills. However, comprehensive screening may be able to weed out the bad apples. Tenant screening, in addition to taking care of maintenance issues, collecting rent, and being compliant with the law, may be a time-consuming and laborious process but it will save you from much trouble in the future.
