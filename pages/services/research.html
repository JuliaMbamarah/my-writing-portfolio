OPEC as a Global Business Influencer

History of OPEC

In 1960, during the time of decolonization and creation of many new independent states across the developing world, government officials from the five founding countries of OPEC met at the Baghdad conference held from 10th-14th of September, to discuss strategies to secure the best price of crude oil available for major oil corporations. This was in response to the MOC unilateral action of cutting down oil prices. Its headquarters was originally set up in Geneva, Switzerland, but after five years, the location was changed to Vienna, Austria. The five founding countries of OPEC in 1960 include: Iraq, Iran, Venezuela, Kuwait, and Saudi Arabia.

Aims and Objectives

OPEC was set up as a means to devise strategies to challenge the problem being brought about by the Multinational oil companies. Known as the ‘Seven sisters’, MOC used to monopolize the oil market. The aims and objectives of OPEC include:

  • To ensure price stability – In the absence of OPEC, Individual Oil-producing countries in the bid to compete with each other, set lower prices as well as maximize production, resulting in more global demand leading to unsustainable depreciation of natural resources.
  • To reduce oil price volatility – OPEC ensures that the price target is being met by setting a limit and an excited target for daily production to ensure that there is no fluctuations in supply that could lead to volatility in oil price.
  • Adjust world oil supply – For oil shortage due to occurrences in some parts of the world OPEC ensures there is increased production in other parts to meet up to global energy production expectations.

Update

In the light of the global pandemic state of the economy in the earlier months of 2020, the world experienced a general decrease in Oil demand.

  • DOC participating countries agreed to hold monthly OPEC and Non-OPEC Ministerial meetings, ONOMM, to evaluate market conditions and decide on production adjustments for subsequent months. The 15th and most current ONOMM meeting took place via video conference on the 1st of April where adjustments to production levels were made and countries with overproducing volumes were sanctioned. In addition, ways to reduce greenhouse gas emissions were reviewed.
  • The 180th and most recent meeting of the OPEC conference on the global oil demand decrease in the second wave of the pandemic and the forecasted growth by 2021. OPEC also celebrated its 60th Anniversary since its founding in Baghdad, 1960.

OPEC and Canada

Back in 1973, when OPEC placed an embargo on oil production, it was targeted towards five countries: Canada, Japan, Netherlands, United Kingdom, and United States. Canada is the 4th world-largest oil producer; however, the industry has suffered low oil demand due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Its production costs are high, and it exports majorly to the United States at a heavy discount. The Canadian government is working hard to implement policies to help Oil producers rise from the price fall due to the pandemic. OPEC views Canada and the US in opposition to the goals of OPEC. During OPEC’s recent conference, it agreed to cut its Production to 9.7 million barrels per day, about 10 percent of the global oil supply. Canadian oil wells, however, will be mostly shutdown despite OPEC’s deal. Because Canada and other countries such as the US are struggling to increase the oil supply base and OPEC is regulating production to avoid excess supply and increase oil price, there exists a negative relationship between the Canadian Oil Industry and OPEC.

Arguments in support of OPEC:

  • Weak Individual policy changes – Threatening policy changes to OPEC’s survival cannot hold even in the event of a change in leadership because new regimes are more likely to stand by OPEC to increase income rather than take short-term beneficial lone actions.
  • Conservationist outlook – This development was procreated through the gradual devolution of control over production policy from the international oil companies to the local government. The outlook is a shift from profit maximization, that is increased prices and production to profit optimization, that is stabilized outputs compensated by price increases.

Learning Theories

Behaviourism

Introduction

Behaviourism is one of the four major theories of learning, propounded by John Watson in his classic paper, ‘Psychology as the behaviorist view it’, which was published in 1913. It is a learning theory that proposes the ideology that all behaviors are learned through an interaction with the environment in a process known as conditioning. It is concerned only with stimuli response that can be observed and discounts any independent activities of the mind. Behaviourism had evolved from the tradition of Empiricists who believed in objective and verifiable data. It was the dominant school of thought between the 1920’s and the mid 1950’s.

Strengths of Behaviourism:

Strengths Examples
Behaviourism is based on observable behavior; therefore it is a powerful tool used in research for providing clear evidence. It provides quantifiable data, clear evidence/proof, and detailed explanations backed up by research.
It is scientific and can be replicated. It uses scientific research methods which can be objective, observable, and quantifiable. It is used for laboratory experiments, human and animal conditioning.
It is useful for behavior modification. It has therapeutic techniques that can address behavioral problems in both children and adults. Forms of these behavioral modifications include: intense behavioral intervention, discrete trial training, and behavior analysis.
It is useful in therapy, education, parenting, and childcare. Examples of its applications include: token economies, aversion therapy, systematic desensitization, modeling, contingency management.

Weaknesses of Behaviourism:

Weaknesses Examples
It is a one-dimensional approach. It is superficial in explaining learning and behavior as it only considers what is observable and measurable. It only considers stimuli from the environmental effect.
It does not account for biological influences. Behaviourism neglects the biological psychology of the human body as a key determinant factor in influencing human actions. Some of these neglected factors include: the power of the brain, genetics, neurotransmitters, hormones.
It is reductionist and does not consider moods, thoughts, or feelings. It reduces human behavior to just memory and attention. Some of these neglected human behaviors include: feelings, desires, thoughts, intentionality, self-reactiveness.
It does not explain learning. It doesn’t take into account the capabilities of the human full functioning. These abilities include: thinking, decision making, language, problem solving.

Impacts of Behaviourism

  • The behaviorist theory is employed even in the modern classroom. Students who perform well are rewarded for their good behavior through commendations, praises, and good grades. The impact this application has is to make other students strive for better performance. Examples of these include: rewards revisions, question and answer.
  • In the same vein, students who do not perform well or who exhibit some attributes that are considered unruly will be punished in one form or another. The impact this has is to make students avoid circumstances that would result in those undesirable feelings. Examples of these include: scolding, detention, exclusion, and sometimes even isolation.
  • It is employed in teaching students that the more they practice, the better their performance would be, and the more praise that they will receive for such high performance, in a form of reinforcement. The impact this has is to make students continuously repeat tasks like taking quizzes, studying to improve test scores and get better grades. Examples of these include: setting daily/weekly/monthly tasks, drills, quick feedbacks, start of lesson routines.
