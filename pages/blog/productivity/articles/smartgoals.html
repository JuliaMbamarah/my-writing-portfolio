How to Set SMART Goals: The Ultimate Guide

Date: November 1, 2023 | Author: Julia Mbamarah

Permit me to say that everyone has goals. Not a lot of people set these goals. If you’ve successfully been able to turn an idea or a task into a goal then congratulations! However, you should know that a goal cannot be sustainable if it has not been properly defined. Mind you, the word is ‘properly defined’.

If you still have the same wavering goal that you’ve had for years; say, you made a decision to brush twice a day and till now you’ve made minimal progress because you have continued to relapse, then you most certainly do not have a defined goal.

If you’re not setting SMART goals, then you might as well stop setting goals altogether because those will get you nowhere. I like to define SMART goals as everything you need to realize your goal’s potential. Source

SMART goals are goals that are:

  • Specific
  • Measurable
  • Attainable
  • Relevant
  • Time-based

I’ll be explaining how exactly to set these types of goals.

One – Specific goals

You need to start now to define specific goals. Hit the nail on the head and leave no information behind. This is probably the most important part of setting your goals and the part that most people neglect. Start with clearly stating out the information surrounding the goal you’re trying to achieve.

Say you’re looking towards starting your own Tech start-up, then a specific goal may be something in the lines of ‘I want to gain the necessary economic, business, financial and marketing knowledge and experience I would require to run a successful company.’ Another example could be in the lines of, ‘I want to learn how to code in python so I can employ it in learning ‘Machine learning’.

The trick is in clearly capturing what you need to achieve what you want.

Two – Measurable goals

How many times have you heard the saying that you cannot evaluate what you cannot measure? The reality cannot be far from the truth. This is where most people mess up their plans. Measuring your goals is the only way to checkmate yourself and access your progress.

For example, in your quest to start your own Tech company, after you must have clearly specified your goal, you need to have certain conditions set in place to enable you to actively measure your progress. Another example could be in the lines of, ‘I will practice what I’ve learnt at the end of each day’s work to see if I’m making progress’.

Three – Achievable goals

Don’t fall into the trap of unrealistic goal setting. If there is no how, then can be no activity. You know a goal is unrealistic when there is no reasonable possible means of achieving it. This may be due to limitations in resources, skill, time or energy.

In your quest, let no one stop you from aiming for the stars and stretching beyond what you feel is your limit; however know that if you set goals that are way beyond your reach in terms of any of those factors we discussed above or more, then you’re probably going to end up with frustrated goals.

Four – Relevant goals

This is the aspect that should matter most to you. You must determine how the goal you’re trying to achieve matters to your broader perspectives. Most of our goals are short term, at least after we must have broken the long-term goal into achievable milestones.

If a present goal doesn’t seem worthwhile, then you most likely defined the wrong goal. They should align with your bigger picture.

For example, for our Tech Start-up, a goal such as ‘expanding services and products’ may not be as worthwhile for it in its seed stage. Few years into its growth, then that would be a fantastic target relevant to starting a successful Tech Start-up. Also, in your Python learning journey for Machine learning, setting a goal to focus on python for web development may not necessarily be relevant to your goal of applying python to machine learning.

You must always consider the relevancy of your goal to the bigger picture.

Five – Time-based goals

If you’ve learned anything from time boxing, then it should be the relevance of setting deadlines for specific tasks you want to achieve and defining them within time boxes. The same knowledge is applied here. For you to complete your goal definition, you must assign a reasonable timeframe to your goal in the order that you want it to be completed. What this does is to create a sense of urgency accompanied by motivation to complete the task in due time.

For example, when defining your specific goal for your Tech start-up, you could say something in the lines of ‘For the knowledge and skill in the different niches that I have clearly defined to attain, I will aim to complete all the necessary materials and training in a month, assigning each week for each niche, and setting 3 hours in my day to see to this’. You could go further to state the specific time of the day you want to dedicate to achieving your goals.




Conclusion

SMART is a very effective method to see that those goals of yours do not go unachieved. It practically sets you up for success by providing you with Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, and Time-bound goals. If you have discarded this method then it’s time to bring it back because you’re heading nowhere without it while your goals are waiting to be accomplished.
